Why tires are so bad for the environment

Have you ever heard that “whoosh!” sound cars make? That’s the sound of your tires scraping on roads. Here’s why that has big consequences, as well as what you can do to help.


Part 1: Pollution

Humans as a species, throw out on average, 3 MILLION tires every DAY1. For reference that’s 35 tires every second.



Luckily, there are lots of companies working on recycling tires!2 Only- they’re just recycling what’s left of the tire after it’s been used. While getting your tires replaced have you ever noticed how a good chunk of your old tires is just... gone? In a best case scenario 80% of the tread has been removed before tyres are replaced3. So where did all that extra tire go? Or more accurately where didn’t it go? A typical passenger tire is composed of 30 different types of synthetic rubber. When driving these rubber particles are sprayed into the air! It’s mixes into the soil! It’s washed into rivers, lakes and into the ocean4! Mother nature is getting tired because everywhere there’s a road, there’s toxic rubber being sprayed into the environment, and no, electric cars don’t solve this problem.



We can’t just look at tires though, we have to look at the infrastructure that supports them. Roads. These asphalt strips are constantly degrading and need to be resealed every decade, and completely rebuilt every 30 years5. For a particularly bad example, America spends 231 billion dollars on roads each year6. Money that could be going to say… public transport, social housing or stopping CO2 getting into our atmosphere.

Part 2: Efficiency

Tires are designed to be large and grippy. This is called static friction and it’s an absolute necessity because without this, cars would slip and kill a lot of people. More than they already do. So, the static friction of tires make cars great at starting and stopping, but of course there’s a downside, when they start moving they generate a whole lot of friction slowing them down. This is kinetic friction also known as “rolling resistance” and this friction uses up energy that is then wasted as heat and noise7 . The average engine is about 40% efficient. Pretty terrible already. When driving however, up to 30% of the energy that makes it to the wheels is used then used up because of tires8, which means electric cars aren’t immune to this either. The range listed on brand new electric cars were likely calculated using ultra-low rolling resistance tires in ideal conditions. The electricity wasted by tires could be used to decarbonise other sectors of the economy, but right now all this extra energy and by extension CO2 is just wasted as heat and… noise pollution.




Part 3: Noise

When you think about the loudest part of the car most people would say it’s the engine right? One of the big selling points for electric cars is how quiet they are but- the noise is the same to everyone else outside of a soundproofed vehicle. The rolling noise of the tires becomes louder than the propulsion noise of the engine at speeds over 55kmph. So, most of the time it’s the tires that are to blame for why cars are loud. Believe it or not, this used to be a much bigger problem in the past. That’s because this regular pattern of treads causes the tires to make noise at the same frequency and it was unbearably loud. In order to solve this, the brand-new field of pitch sequence engineering was born. Engineers experimented with different patterns, to take that single loud peak of noise, and distribute it across different frequencies. So modern tires are specifically designed to make white noise. Unfortunately, this second type of noise can be seriously detrimental to health. It causes sleep disturbances so tires might literally be why you’re always tired. It’s also been shown to increase anxiety, especially in children! And because of this it’s significantly increased the rate of chronic heart failure, the number 1 killer of humans. So, while modern tires are less loud, the total amount of noise, (the area under the curve) is still the same. The same amount of energy is wasted, and the same amount of pollution is sprayed into the environment.
