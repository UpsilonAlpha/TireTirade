Visualizing the environmental and social impacts of tyred vehicles

Introduction

Tires are regularly undergoing abrasive friction with roads, leading to constant air and noise pollution. Furthermore, at the end of their short lifecycle, they are disposed of, generating mountains of waste each day. However, the general public is mostly unaware of this impact. This has major consequences, however there are also various methods to mitigate these negative effects. This report covers different ways to understand the problems that tires have, as well as strategies have gone into mitigating it.


Part 1: Pollution

Humans as a species, throw out an estimated 1 billion tires in a year according to the World Business Council for Sustainable Development Managing End-of-Life Tires1. This number is difficult to comprehend and not useful for understanding the scope of the problem. However, for the sake of understanding this rate can be interpolated to obtain the much easier to grasp statistic, of approximately 32 tires per second. The resulting visualization was created using a particle-based physics simulation using a 3D model of a tire, showing what 32 tires per second would visually look like.


Figure 1: Physical visualization of the interpolated rate of tire disposal worldwide


Luckily, there are companies and agencies working on recycling end of life tires (ELTs)2. In Australia, government and industry have collaborated to make great strides in recycling ELTs. This is why the rate of recycling has drastically increased since 2017. Data for this visualization was obtained from the 2022 National Waste Database3. This data had to first be processed. Data concerning tire waste was isolated from the rest of the database. Then this data was cleaned by removing commas and converting numerical data into usable values. Then a subset was selected from 2012 to 2021.


Figure 2: Chloropleth map of the amount of tire waste in tonnes being recycled in each Australian state over time.



In the years preceding 2016 very little recycling can be seen. However, between 2017 and 2018 this amount jumps drastically, especially in Queensland and New South Wales. This corresponds with a decrease in ELTs going into landfill between 2017 and 2018 as seen below.




Figure 3: Stacked line graph of the amount of tire waste in tonnes going to landfill in each Australian state over time.



This drastic decrease that can be seen in Figure 3 is due to a push by federal government policy2. This has greatly reduced the environmental impact of end-of-life tires by preventing microplastics entering the environment from landfill. However, recycling only occurs on what’s left of the tire after it’s been used. In a best-case scenario 80% of the tire tread has been removed before the tyres are replaced4.

So where did all that extra material go? A 2017 study on concentrations of tire wear microplastics and other traffic-derived non-exhaust particles in the road environment, conducted in the Netherlands discovered the pathways by which the majority of tire microplastics enter the environment5. When driving, due to abrasion with the road, rubber particles are sprayed into the air, mix into the soil, then wash into rivers, lakes and into the ocean. These microplastics are sprayed into the environment, regardless of whether a car is electric or powered by a internal combustion engine.


Figure 4: Bar chart of the tonnes of microplastics entering soil, water, air and sewage systems per annum in the Netherlands.

As can be seen, the majority of microplastics are absorbed into the soil. However, a small proportion is removed via runoff or released into the atmosphere. This bar chart was constructed using data from a list of values found in the aforementioned 2017 study, determining the approximate quantity of microplastics found in a road environment and in sewage treatment plants per year. Percentage error bars were added based on the errors given by researchers in the study. It should also be noted that this is simply how the microplastics enter the environment, and that after a time, a large proportion is theorized to enter the ocean5.

This raises the question, what do these microplastics consist of? A typical passenger tire is composed of over 30 different types of ingredients6. This is difficult to list or display intuitively. Therefore, a sunburst chart was used to convey the proportions of ingredients that go into making a tire. This data was gathered from a meta-analysis on studies into the composition of an average car tyre.


Figure 5: Sunburst chart of the different proportions of ingredients in kilograms that compose the average tire.

(hover for more detail)




The majority of a tire is composed of rubber and dense filler. Interestingly, a significant amount of antiozonants and antioxidants are added, around 0.61 kilograms. The most commonly used chemical for this purpose, 6PPD, is highly toxic to fish7, which makes this formulation concerning.

Clicking on this chart lets one focus on the proportions of a specific type of material. Furthermore, the sunburst plot circumvents the typical limitations of a pie chart as hovering over sections outputs the total weight (in kilograms) of that ingredient present in the average tire. This compacts a lot of categorical and numerical data intuitively into a small area, giving both an qualitative and quantitative understanding of tire composition.

Part 2: Efficiency

Tires are designed to be large and have high grip. This is called static friction and it’s an absolute necessity because without this, cars would have no traction and cause more accidents than they already do. The static friction of tires makes cars great at starting and stopping, but there’s a downside. When they are in motion they generate a whole lot of friction, slowing them down. This is kinetic friction also known as “rolling resistance” and this friction uses up energy that is then wasted as heat and noise. The average combustion engine is about 40% efficient9. When driving however, up to half of the energy that makes it to the wheels is consumed by the tires9, which means electric cars aren’t immune to this either. This extra energy and by extension CO2 is wasted as heat and noise pollution.

Figure 6: Sankey diagram of different ways energy is lost after reaching the trasmission



This diagram shows the flow of energy once it makes it past the car's transmission which applies for both combustion and electric cars. The data was gathered from the Australian National University, from a model of how energy is spent in a car9. This gives a visual representation of how much energy is lost via drag and rolling resistance compared to something more familiar like air resistance and braking. Most of this energy is then lost has heat, but a small amount also becomes noise pollution.

Part 3: Noise

Most people would assume the engine is the loudest part of a car. In fact, one of the big selling points for electric cars is how quiet they are. However, noise levels are very similar outside of a soundproofed vehicle. The rolling noise of the tires becomes louder than the engine at speeds over 30kmph. So, most of the time it’s the tires that are to blame for why cars are loud. Believe it or not, this used to be a much bigger problem in the past, when tires used to make a high frequency hum. This clip was gathered from a car in the 1920s10.



That’s because this regular pattern of treads causes the tires to make noise at the same frequency and it was unbearably loud. In order to solve this, the brand-new field of pitch sequence engineering was born. Engineers experimented with different patterns, to take that single loud peak of noise, and distribute it across different frequencies. So modern tires are specifically designed to make white noise 15. This clip was collected personally using a microphone on Metroad 5 Brisbane.



The volume of these recordings was first normalized, then a fast fourier transform algorithm was used to extract the frequencies from these recordings. Due to the noisiness of the real-world recordings a Savitzky-Golay smoothing algorithm was also applied. This produces a smoother spectrum of the frequencies obtained. The animation below shows the transition between the sharp harmonic sound produced by the 1920s tire to the smooth "white noise" of a modern tire.

Figure 7: A fourier transform of the sound made by tires manufatured in the 1920s and modern tires



Unfortunately, this white noise, while less noticeable, can be seriously detrimental to health. It causes sleep disturbances and might be the reason why people in cities are always tired. This has also been shown to increase anxiety, especially in children. Because of these stress-altering effect tire noise has also significantly increased the rate of chronic heart failure, the number 1 killer of humans10. So, while modern tires are less "loud", they can still have serious negative health effects.

A Tireless Future?

While it may seem like tyres are here to stay, there are still many other ways to minimize their negative impacts. One alternative is more frequent use of rail transport. Trains also have a very high capacity, further reducing car use13. Railway lines don't shed microplastics are they are made using mostly only steel, wood, and concrete. Due to this rail infrastructure can also be more easily recycled compared to tyres. They are also far more efficient in terms of rolling resistance as steel wheels are used instead of tires. On the other hand bikes use tires, but they are smaller and under less mechanical load. Bike infrastructure and riding is also much more energy efficient14. Buses are also viable because they use fewer tires to transport far more people13. A final benefit is that all these forms of transport have lower rates of fatalities and serious injuries per year (FSI)12. These factors can be seen in combination be seen below.


Figure 8: A bubble chart of the fatal and significant injuries against grams of CO2 per kilometer with bubble size representing capacity on a 3.5 meter wide corridor

Data was gathered from three separate studies for this final visualization. From one 2019 study, the emissions of CO2 in grams per kilometer were obtained14. From a 2022 study the FSI per annum for each mode of transport in the Australian state of Victoria was obtained13. Then finally, from a 1991 study, the number of people that are transported using a given method through a 3.5 meter wide corridor per hour, also known as the "corridor capacity" was obtained.

This figure can therefore be qualitatively split into 4 main quadrants. Cars have the highest emissions per kilometre and the highest number of FSIs per year compared to other forms of transport, putting them in the top right quadrant: high emissions and high FSI. Pedestrians and cyclists both all fall under low emission and low FSI (bottom left). Buses have high emissions per kilometre and FSI. No form of transport has low emissions and high FSI. Trains have higher emissions than pedestrians and cyclists but lower FSI putting them in between the top and bottom left quadrant. This figure also shows that, cars have one of the smallest capacities in a 3.5-meter-wide corridor. Pedestrians, cyclists, and trains have a relatively high corridor capacity as more individuals can travel through the same space. Buses have a similar corridor capacity to cars; however, an important caveat is that this figure does not take into account bi-articulated buses and separated busways which greatly increases corridor capacity. What this figure shows is while we won’t be able to eliminate tires completely, we can be better using the technology we already have.
